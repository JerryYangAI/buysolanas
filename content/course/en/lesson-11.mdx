## Identifying Scams: Rug Pulls & Phishing

The crypto space offers enormous opportunity, but it also attracts scammers. Understanding common attack patterns is your best defense. This lesson covers the most prevalent scams on Solana and how to protect yourself.

### What Is a Rug Pull?

A rug pull happens when a project's creators suddenly withdraw all liquidity or abandon the project after collecting investor funds. The token price crashes to zero, and investors lose everything.

**Warning signs of a rug pull:**

- **Anonymous team** — no verifiable identities or track record.
- **Locked liquidity claims without proof** — they say liquidity is locked but cannot show the lock contract.
- **Unrealistic promises** — "100x guaranteed" or "risk-free returns."
- **No audit** — the smart contract has never been reviewed by a security firm.
- **Concentrated token supply** — a few wallets hold most of the tokens and can dump at any time.
- **Sudden hype with no substance** — heavy social media promotion but no working product.

### How Phishing Attacks Work

Phishing tricks you into revealing your private keys or approving malicious transactions. Common methods include:

**Fake websites:** Scammers create sites that look identical to legitimate platforms (e.g., "jupltter.ag" instead of "jup.ag"). Always check the URL carefully and bookmark trusted sites.

**Discord and Telegram scams:** Fake "support" accounts DM you claiming there is a problem with your wallet. They direct you to a fake site that asks you to "verify" by entering your seed phrase.

**Malicious token airdrops:** You receive unknown tokens in your wallet with names like "Visit-claim-reward.com." Interacting with these tokens can trigger malicious smart contracts. **Never interact with tokens you did not expect to receive.**

**Fake transaction approvals:** A dApp asks you to approve a transaction that actually grants unlimited access to your tokens. Always read what you are signing before confirming.

### Verification Methods

Before interacting with any project or token:

1. **Check the contract address** — verify it on the official project website or CoinGecko, not from social media links.
2. **Use block explorers** — look up the token on SolScan to check holder distribution and transaction history.
3. **Review social proof** — does the project have a real community with organic engagement, or just bots?
4. **Verify on Jupiter** — Jupiter has a strict token verification process. Verified tokens show a checkmark badge.
5. **Check liquidity depth** — use Birdeye or DEXScreener to see if the token has sufficient liquidity.

### Your Safety Checklist

Use this checklist before investing in any new token or project:

- [ ] Is the team publicly known and verifiable?
- [ ] Has the smart contract been audited?
- [ ] Is liquidity locked, and can you verify the lock?
- [ ] Is the token verified on Jupiter or a major aggregator?
- [ ] Are there more than 500 unique holders?
- [ ] Does the project have a working product (not just promises)?
- [ ] Have you checked the contract address from an official source?
- [ ] Is the website URL correct (no typosquatting)?

### What to Do If You Suspect a Scam

1. **Stop interacting** — do not approve any more transactions.
2. **Revoke approvals** — use a tool like revoke.cash to remove token approvals you have granted.
3. **Move funds** — transfer remaining assets to a fresh wallet if your current wallet may be compromised.
4. **Report** — flag the project on the marketplace or platform where you found it.
5. **Warn others** — share your experience (with evidence) to help the community.

### The Golden Rules

- **Never share your seed phrase** — no legitimate service will ever ask for it.
- **If it sounds too good to be true, it probably is.**
- **Take your time** — scammers create urgency. Legitimate opportunities will still be there tomorrow.
